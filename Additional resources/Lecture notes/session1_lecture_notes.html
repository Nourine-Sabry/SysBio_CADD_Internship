The Reductionist Problem
0:02 — 12 min
What the slide says
Each biologist specialises in one area of biology. Neither of them are wrong, but neither tell the whole story. Biology alone isn't enough — we need physics, math, chemistry, and computer science. Illustration: two people arguing about whether a symbol on the ground is a 6 or a 9.
The 6/9 analogy — explain this fully, don't rush it

Look at the illustration. Two people standing over a number painted on the ground. One sees a 6. One sees a 9. They're arguing. Who's right? [pause] They're both right. They're also both incomplete. Each person is observing accurately from their own position — but neither has access to the full picture.

This is exactly how biology has worked for most of the twentieth century. One scientist studies TNF-alpha. Another studies IL-6. Another studies AKT. Each of them produces careful, accurate, peer-reviewed science. But none of them can tell you how TNF-alpha and IL-6 and AKT interact with each other to produce the disease state we call rheumatoid arthritis. They're all looking at the same ground from different angles, arguing about whether it's a 6 or a 9.

Now — what could they do to resolve this? The slide gives you four answers. Let's go through them quickly because each one maps directly to something we do in systems biology.

Write on whiteboard as you say each one
Communicate perspectives → in biology: share your data, publish, collaborate across specialities
Gather more context → in biology: collect data from multiple sources, multiple experiments, multiple patients
Consult an independent source → in biology: use curated databases that aggregate knowledge (STRING, DisGeNET, GeneCards)
Accept one observation isn't enough → in biology: this is exactly why we do multi-dataset analyses and why we have Venn diagrams
Continue

The reductionist approach — breaking biology into its smallest pieces and studying each piece in isolation — was essential for building the knowledge base we have today. We know what individual proteins do because of decades of reductionist science. But when you want to understand disease — which is almost never caused by one protein failing in isolation — reductionism runs into a hard limit.

A cancer cell doesn't just have one broken gene. It has dozens of interacting mutations, dysregulated pathways, cross-talk between oncogenes and tumour suppressors. If you study each one in isolation, you'll never understand why the cancer behaves the way it does. You need a different framework. That framework is systems biology.

The third bullet on this slide is the key one: biology alone isn't enough. We need physics to understand molecular forces. We need math to model interactions. We need chemistry to understand drug-receptor binding. And we need computer science to handle the scale. A human PPI network has over 100,000 known interactions. You cannot analyse that in Excel. You need computational tools and mathematical concepts from graph theory.

Discussion prompt — 2 minutes max
Question to ask: "Can you think of a disease where studying one gene or one protein clearly isn't enough to explain what's happening?" Let them answer. Likely responses: cancer, diabetes, Alzheimer's. Affirm and use their example — "exactly, and that's exactly the type of disease systems biology was built for."

Systems Biology — Definition
0:20 — 10 min
What the slide says
Systems biology is a way of thinking before it is a set of tools. Interdisciplinary field, computational and mathematical models, complex interactions within biological systems. Holistic approach. Studies emergent properties. Pipeline: Partial observation → Different interpretations → Conflict → Reflection → Integration → Complete understanding.
The most important sentence on this slide

The first bullet is the most important sentence in this slide and possibly in this whole session: "Systems biology is a way of thinking before it is a set of tools." Please write that down.

I'm going to be teaching you tools over the coming weeks — Cytoscape, STRING, AutoDock Vina, SwissADME. Tools are learnable in an afternoon. The way of thinking takes longer. And it's the thinking that makes the difference between someone who can run a pipeline and someone who understands what the pipeline is actually doing.

So what is the thinking? It's this: instead of asking "what does protein X do?", you ask "how does protein X's interactions with all its partners create a disease state?" You move from studying parts to studying relationships. The parts are still important — but they only make sense in context.

On emergent properties — this needs explaining

The slide mentions "emergent properties" — this is a term you'll see everywhere in systems biology, so let me explain it with a concrete example. Take water. Hydrogen and oxygen are both gases. Neither is wet. But combine them in the right structure and you get wetness — a property that neither component has individually. Wetness is an emergent property of the interaction between H and O molecules.

The same principle applies in biology. TNF-alpha alone doesn't cause joint destruction. IL-6 alone doesn't cause synovial hyperplasia. But the interaction between TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-1β, and a dozen other proteins in the context of a synovial joint — that interaction produces rheumatoid arthritis. The disease is an emergent property of the network. You cannot understand or treat it by studying any single node.

Write the pipeline on the board and explain each step
Partial observation → Different interpretations → Conflict → Reflection → Integration → Complete understanding

Say: "This is the scientific method, reframed for systems biology. We start with incomplete data — always. Different people analyse it differently — always. The conflict forces us to think harder. Reflection means we question our assumptions. Integration means we combine all the evidence. And complete understanding is asymptotic — we never fully get there, but each iteration gets us closer."
Analogy to use if they look confused
Think of a city's traffic system. A single traffic light doesn't determine whether the city has traffic jams. It's the emergent behaviour of thousands of lights, drivers, road layouts, and time-of-day patterns interacting. A systems biologist studying a city wouldn't count cars at one intersection. They'd build a network of all intersections and model the flow through the whole system.

Biological Networks & Graph Theory
0:30 — 8 min
What the slide says
Mathematical modelling of complex biological systems as networks. Nodes (vertices) = biological entities. Edges (arrows) = interactions. Translates intricate biological data into structures. Deals with properties of graphs. Allows representation and analysis using graph theory tools.
What to say — make this very concrete

Graph theory is a branch of mathematics that dates back to 1736 — Euler solving the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem. For our purposes, all you need to know is this: a graph is a set of dots connected by lines. In mathematics, the dots are called vertices and the lines are called edges. In biology, we call them nodes and edges, but it's the same concept.

Why does this matter? Because it gives us a mathematical framework for studying biology. Instead of describing protein interactions in prose — "protein A binds to protein B which then phosphorylates protein C" — we represent the whole system as a graph and use mathematical tools to analyse it. We can ask quantitative questions: which node is most central? Which edge is most critical? Which cluster of nodes are most interconnected? These are mathematical questions with mathematical answers, and they're much more rigorous than intuition.

Key Concept
What goes where in a biological network
  • Node = biological entity: in a PPI network, a node is a protein. In a metabolic network, nodes are metabolites or enzymes. In a gene regulatory network, nodes are genes and transcription factors.
  • Edge = relationship: in a PPI network, an edge means these two proteins physically interact. In a metabolic network, a directed edge means this enzyme catalyses this reaction. In a signalling network, a directed edge with an arrowhead means activation; with a flat end means repression.
  • The same protein can appear in multiple network types — AKT1, for example, is a node in PPI networks, signalling networks, and metabolic networks simultaneously.

Graph Theory — Properties of Graphs (Nodes, Edges, Repression/Activation)
0:38 — 5 min
What to say

This slide introduces two basic building blocks. A node represents an entity — a protein, gene, metabolite, or cell type depending on what type of network you're building. Two nodes connected by an edge are called neighbours in graph theory language. This matters because many centrality calculations are based on counting neighbours, or counting neighbours of neighbours.

Edges represent relationships. But not all edges are the same. In a PPI network, edges are symmetric — if protein A binds B, then B also binds A. But in regulatory networks, edges have direction and sign. A directed arrow with a standard arrowhead means activation: gene A activates gene B. A directed arrow with a flat end means repression: gene A represses gene B. The sign matters enormously for understanding how perturbations propagate through the network.

Point to the flat-ended edge and the arrowhead as you describe them. This simple drawing will be referenced multiple times as the session continues.

Types of Edges
0:43 — 4 min
Keep this tight — 4 types, 1 sentence each

Directed: The edge has a source and a target — information flows one way. Used in signalling networks, gene regulatory networks, metabolic networks. "A phosphorylates B" is different from "B phosphorylates A".

Undirected: The relationship is symmetric — no defined source or target. Used in PPI networks where binding is mutual. "A binds B" is the same as "B binds A".

Bidirectional: Directed but in both directions simultaneously. Represents feedback — A activates B which in turn activates A. Common in regulatory loops and co-activation scenarios.

Regulation arrows: Directed edges with an additional sign encoding whether the interaction is activating (positive) or inhibitory (negative). The colours in the figure — red for inhibition, blue for activation — are a common convention but not universal across tools. Always check the legend.

Basic Network Components — Directed, Undirected, Weighted, Bipartite
0:47 — 6 min
What the slide says
Directed / Undirected / Weighted / Bipartite — with visual examples of each.
The one that needs extra explanation is Weighted and Bipartite

Directed and undirected you already know. Weighted means that edges carry a numerical value — not just "are these two proteins connected?" but "how strongly are they connected?" In STRING, every interaction has a combined score between 0 and 1 representing the confidence of the interaction based on experimental evidence, co-expression, text mining, and other sources. When we import a STRING network into Cytoscape, we'll use these weights to set edge thickness — thicker edge = more confident interaction.

Bipartite is a graph where nodes fall into two distinct categories, and edges only go between categories — never within them. A classic biological example: transcription factors and their target genes. TFs are one type of node, target genes are another. Edges only go from TFs to targets, never TF-to-TF or target-to-target. Drug-target networks are also bipartite: drugs on one side, proteins on the other.

Quick check question
Ask: "If I build a PPI network from STRING with confidence scores — is it directed or undirected? Is it weighted or unweighted?" Expected answer: undirected (binding is symmetric), weighted (STRING provides scores). If they get it wrong, correct gently and explain why.

Network Biology — Topology, Small-World, Scale-Free, Transitivity
0:53 — 12 min · this slide needs significant extra explanation
What the slide says
Topology = arrangement of nodes and edges. Can apply to whole network or individual nodes/edges. Three main characteristics: small-world effect (~6 steps), scale-free (hubs), transitivity (communities).
Gap in the slide — explain scale-free networks fully
The slide mentions scale-free networks but doesn't explain what makes a network scale-free or why it matters. This is critical for understanding why hubs exist and why they're drug targets. Spend ~5 minutes here.
On small-world effect

Small-world effect: you may have heard of "six degrees of separation" — the idea that any two people on Earth are connected through at most six social links. Biological networks show the same property. In the human PPI network, you can get from any protein to any other protein through on average 6 hops or fewer. This is remarkable given the network has over 20,000 nodes. It means that a perturbation — a mutation, a drug, a pathogen — can propagate across the entire network very quickly, which is why diseases and drug effects are so systemic.

On scale-free networks — this is the critical concept, take your time

Scale-free is a property of the degree distribution — the distribution of how many connections each node has. In a random network — like dots connected by randomly drawn lines — most nodes have roughly the same number of connections. The distribution looks like a bell curve centred around the average degree.

Biological networks don't look like this. In a scale-free network, the vast majority of nodes have very few connections, and a tiny minority of nodes have an enormous number of connections. The distribution follows a power law — which on a log-log plot looks like a straight line — and has no characteristic scale, which is where the name comes from.

The biological implications are profound. Those few extremely well-connected nodes are your hubs — proteins like TP53, AKT1, TNF-alpha. They interact with hundreds of other proteins. If you disrupt a hub, you disrupt a huge fraction of the network. This is why these proteins are so often implicated in disease, and why they're such attractive drug targets. It's also why cancer is so hard to treat — tumours often exploit hub proteins, and disrupting hubs has massive off-target effects throughout the network.

Draw on whiteboard: two degree distributions side by side — one bell curve labelled "random network", one power-law curve labelled "scale-free network". Under the power-law: most nodes have degree 1–2, very few nodes have degree 50+. Those high-degree nodes are hubs. Draw one hub node with many edges radiating out.
On transitivity

Transitivity, also called clustering, refers to the tendency of biological networks to form communities — groups of nodes that are more connected to each other than to the rest of the network. Think of it as "friends of friends tend to be friends". In biology, proteins that function in the same pathway or complex tend to interact with each other more than with random other proteins. This is what produces modules — the dense sub-graphs we'll identify using MCODE in Session 3.

Network Topology — Degree, Degree Distribution, Clustering Coefficient, Density
1:05 — 8 min
What the slide says
Degree (k) = number of edges per node. In directed: in-degree and out-degree. Degree distribution P(k) = probability a random node has degree k. Clustering coefficient (C) = tendency to form cliques. Network density = actual edges / maximum possible edges.
Gap — degree distribution and clustering coefficient need intuitive explanation
The slide states these definitions but doesn't make them intuitive. Degree distribution especially needs a worked example. Clustering coefficient also needs a numeric illustration.
On degree

Degree is the simplest topological property — just count how many edges connect to a node. A node with degree 1 has one neighbour. TP53 in the human PPI network has a degree of several hundred — it interacts with hundreds of other proteins. In directed networks, we split this into in-degree (edges coming in — things that regulate or activate this protein) and out-degree (edges going out — things this protein regulates or activates).

Degree is how we identify hubs. In your capstone project, after you build the network in Cytoscape, you'll run Network Analyzer and it will calculate the degree of every node. You'll then compute the mean degree and standard deviation, and set a threshold: any node with degree greater than mean + 2 standard deviations is a hub. That's the operationalisation of the hub concept.

On degree distribution — needs a worked example

Degree distribution P(k) answers the question: if I pick a random node in this network, what is the probability it has exactly k connections? For a random network, this follows a Poisson distribution and peaks near the average degree. For a scale-free network, it follows a power law: P(k) is proportional to k to the power of minus-gamma, where gamma is typically between 2 and 3 for biological networks.

Why does this matter practically? Because if your network is scale-free, you know that most proteins are poorly connected, and a handful are extremely connected. That distribution tells you where to focus. It also tells you the network is robust to random failure — if you randomly remove proteins, you're more likely to hit low-degree nodes, and the network stays connected. But it's fragile to targeted attacks — remove the hubs and the network fragments. This is the vulnerability we exploit in drug discovery.

On clustering coefficient — give a numeric example

The clustering coefficient of a node measures: of all the possible connections between its neighbours, what fraction actually exist? If node A has 4 neighbours — B, C, D, E — there are 6 possible edges between those 4 nodes. If 4 of those 6 exist, the clustering coefficient is 4/6 = 0.67. A value of 1 means all neighbours are connected to each other — a clique. A value of 0 means none of the neighbours are connected to each other.

Proteins in tight functional complexes — like ribosomal proteins or proteasome components — have very high clustering coefficients. Signalling hubs that receive input from many different pathways tend to have lower clustering coefficients because their neighbours don't all interact with each other.

C(node) = (number of edges between neighbours) / (k × (k−1) / 2) where k = degree of the node
On network density — keep brief

Network density is the global version of clustering coefficient — for the whole network. It's the ratio of actual edges to the maximum possible edges. A complete graph (every node connected to every other) has density 1. A tree (minimum connections to keep everything connected) has density approaching 0. Real biological networks are sparse — typically less than 1% of possible edges exist — which is why they look like scattered webs rather than solid blobs.

Centrality Measures — Degree, Betweenness, Closeness
1:13 — 10 min · most important conceptual slide in the session
What the slide says
Degree centrality = connections (hubs, local measure). Betweenness centrality = how often on shortest paths (bottlenecks, controls information flow). Closeness centrality = average distance to all other nodes. Figure shows hub-bottleneck, non-hub-bottleneck, hub-non-bottleneck, non-hub-non-bottleneck nodes.
Start with why we need three measures, not just degree

You might wonder: if we can already identify hubs by degree, why do we need betweenness and closeness? This is the key insight of the centrality concept: degree tells you how popular a node is, but it doesn't tell you how important it is for the network's function. Those are different things.

Imagine a road network. A major intersection in a city centre has high degree — many roads meet there. But there might be a single bridge connecting two halves of the city that has degree 2 — only two roads — yet every route between the two halves passes through it. Remove the bridge and the city is cut in half. Remove the busy intersection and the city adjusts. The bridge has low degree but enormous betweenness. In biological terms, that bridge is a bottleneck.

Analogy for betweenness centrality
Degree centrality is like measuring how many friends someone has on social media — a measure of local popularity.

Betweenness centrality is like measuring how often you need to go through someone to connect two groups — a measure of being a bridge, a gatekeeper, a broker. A person with few friends but who all belong to different communities has very high betweenness — they're the only connection between worlds.
Go through each measure precisely

Degree centrality: equal to the degree — the number of edges a node has. Normalised by dividing by (N-1) where N is total nodes. High degree = hub = well-connected protein. This is a local measure — it only looks at the immediate neighbourhood, not the rest of the network. Two proteins can have the same degree but completely different positions in the network.

Betweenness centrality: measures how often a node lies on the shortest path between any two other nodes. Calculate all shortest paths in the network. For each node, count in how many of those paths it appears. Normalise. High betweenness = bottleneck = information gatekeeper. If you remove a high-betweenness node, you disconnect previously connected parts of the network. These are the proteins that coordinate signal flow between different modules.

Closeness centrality: measures how close a node is to all other nodes — defined as the inverse of the average shortest path distance to every other node. A protein with high closeness can "reach" every other protein quickly. This is useful for identifying proteins that rapidly propagate signals throughout the network. Less commonly used in capstone-type analyses, but important in disease network studies where you want to identify rapidly-acting regulatory nodes.

Draw the 4 categories on the whiteboard
Draw a 2×2 grid:
High degree, high betweenness → Hub-Bottleneck (most biologically important, most likely disease gene and drug target)
Low degree, high betweenness → Non-hub Bottleneck (bridge node — critical for connectivity, often a scaffolding protein or cross-pathway connector)
High degree, low betweenness → Hub non-bottleneck (popular but contained — many connections within one module, like a ribosomal protein)
Low degree, low betweenness → Non-hub non-bottleneck (peripheral node — not a focus for drug targeting)

Point out: we want hub-bottlenecks as drug targets because disrupting them affects both local connectivity AND information flow between modules.
Common confusion to pre-empt
Students often confuse hubs with hub-bottlenecks. A hub just means high degree. A hub-bottleneck means BOTH high degree AND high betweenness. In the capstone, when they compute hub-bottlenecks, they'll apply both thresholds: degree > mean+2SD AND top 5% betweenness centrality. Only nodes passing BOTH filters are hub-bottlenecks. Emphasise this intersection explicitly.

Ganging Up! — Community, Module, Complex, Motif
1:28 — 7 min
What the slide says
Community/Cluster: more connected within than without → functional modules and protein complexes. Module: functional units, not required to interact simultaneously. Complex: proteins interact at same time and space. Motif: statistically over-represented sub-graphs — correspond to characteristic dynamical responses.
The module vs. complex distinction is often confused — explain carefully

The distinction between a module and a complex is subtle but important. A protein complex means these proteins are physically bound to each other at the same time and in the same cellular compartment — like the ribosome, or the proteasome, or the cohesin complex. They form a stable multi-protein machine.

A functional module is more abstract. The proteins in a module are functionally related — they work together to carry out a biological process — but they don't have to be physically present simultaneously. For example, all the proteins in the "cell cycle G1/S checkpoint" form a functional module: some act at G1, some at the G1/S boundary, some at S phase, but they're all functionally related. You wouldn't find them all physically bound to each other at the same time.

In network analysis, both complexes and modules show up as dense sub-graphs — clumps of highly interconnected nodes. MCODE, which we'll use in Session 3, doesn't distinguish between them — it just finds dense regions. Your biological interpretation of whether a dense region is a complex or a module comes from the literature and from pathway enrichment analysis.

On motifs — keep brief but make it interesting

Motifs are statistically over-represented sub-graphs — specific patterns of connections that appear more often in real biological networks than you'd expect by chance. The classic example is the negative feedback loop: A activates B, B activates C, C inhibits A. This three-node motif appears far more often in gene regulatory networks than random, because it's useful — it creates a damped oscillation that allows a system to respond to a stimulus and then return to baseline. Another common motif is the feed-forward loop, which filters out transient signals and allows only sustained inputs to propagate. Motifs are the "recurring sentences" of biological network grammar.

PPI Networks — Slide 16
What to add beyond the slide
The slide explains what a PPI is. Add: how we know about PPIs. The main experimental techniques are yeast two-hybrid (Y2H), which tests pairs of proteins for physical interaction in vivo; co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP), which pulls down a protein and identifies what binds to it; and mass spectrometry approaches (AP-MS). These experiments are curated in databases like IntAct, BioGRID, and MINT. STRING aggregates all of these plus computational predictions. When you use STRING in Cytoscape next session, the edges represent all the evidence types combined into a confidence score.
Metabolic Networks — Slide 17
What to add beyond the slide
Add: metabolic networks are the most mathematically sophisticated type — they're often used in flux balance analysis (FBA), where you solve for the steady-state flow of metabolites through the network. This allows prediction of which metabolic reactions are essential and which are dispensable. The slide correctly notes that nodes can be either metabolites (the chemicals) or enzymes (the catalysts), and the choice affects the network's structure and what questions you can ask with it.
Genetic Interaction Networks — Slide 18
What to add beyond the slide
Add: genetic interactions are detected by measuring fitness (growth, survival) when two genes are mutated simultaneously and comparing to what you'd expect from the individual mutations. A negative genetic interaction (synthetic lethality) means mutating gene A and gene B together is far more lethal than either alone — they're in parallel pathways compensating for each other. CRISPR screens now allow detection of genetic interactions at scale. Synthetic lethality is a major cancer therapeutic strategy — find what a tumour depends on given its existing mutations, then target that dependency.
Gene Regulatory Networks — Slide 19
What to add beyond the slide
Add: gene regulatory networks are typically inferred from transcriptomic data — RNA-seq or microarray experiments where you measure gene expression across many conditions and use correlation or mutual information to infer which genes regulate which others. ChIP-seq (chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing) can directly identify which genomic loci a transcription factor binds to, enabling more direct construction of TF → target gene edges. TRRUST and Dorothea are databases of known TF-target relationships used as reference networks.
Signalling Networks — Slide 20
What to add beyond the slide
Add: signalling networks are built from curated pathway databases — primarily KEGG, Reactome, and WikiPathways. These databases encode known signalling cascades as directed, signed graphs. For example, in the PI3K-AKT pathway: PI3K phosphorylates PIP2 to PIP3 (activation edge), PIP3 recruits AKT to the membrane (enabling edge), PDK1 phosphorylates and activates AKT (activation edge), AKT phosphorylates and inhibits PTEN (inhibitory edge). The direction and sign of every edge matters for understanding how signals propagate and how perturbations cascade.
Connecting all types together

One important note: in real disease biology, these network types are not separate. A protein that's a hub in the PPI network is likely also a key node in a signalling network and may regulate gene expression in a gene regulatory network. The most comprehensive analyses integrate multiple network types — this is called multi-layer network analysis. For this internship we'll focus on PPI networks because they're the most tractable and the most directly connected to drug target identification. But when you read papers that integrate PPI with signalling or with gene regulatory data, you'll now understand why they're doing that.

Reframe the workflow as a sequence of questions

This workflow slide shows a lot of tool names and database logos, but I want to teach you the logic behind it rather than the tools themselves — we'll cover the specific tools in the next session when we're actually using them.

The workflow answers a series of questions in sequence. Let me walk through them:

Question 1: Which genes are involved in this disease? This is where we start. We query databases of disease-gene associations to get a list of genes relevant to our disease of interest. In Session 2, you'll do this using DisGeNET, GeneCards, and DISEASES. The output is a gene list.

Question 2: How do the proteins encoded by these genes interact? We take the gene list, find the corresponding proteins, and look up their known physical interactions in a PPI database — primarily STRING for this internship. This gives us the edges of our network. The output is a network file we import into Cytoscape.

Question 3: What is the structure of this network? Now we analyse the network topology. We calculate degree, betweenness centrality, clustering coefficient. We identify hubs and bottlenecks. We run MCODE to find modules. The output is a ranked list of hub-bottleneck proteins and a set of functional modules.

Question 4: What do these modules mean biologically? We take each module's gene list and run GO and KEGG enrichment analysis to understand which biological processes and pathways they represent. The output is pathway annotations for each module — the biological interpretation of the topological structure.

Question 5: Which proteins do we target with a drug? We select hub-bottleneck proteins that sit in disease-relevant modules, have known druggable structures, and have published evidence for their role in the disease. This selection, which you'll do in Session 3, hands off directly to the CADD phase.

Write this linear logic on the board — it's the backbone of sessions 1–4
Disease name → Disease gene list → PPI network → Topology analysis → Hub-bottleneck proteins → Module enrichment → Drug target selection
